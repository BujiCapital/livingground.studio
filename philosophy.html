We design landscapes the way nature designs ecosystems

Every Living Ground project is guided by twelve design principles — a framework for creating landscapes that are beautiful, productive, and built to last generations. Not rules. Intelligence.

Three commitments before we draw a single line

These aren't slogans. They're the foundation of every design decision we make.

♢

Care for the land

Every landscape we build improves the soil, conserves water, and creates habitat. Your backyard becomes part of the living ecosystem — not separate from it.

♡

Care for the people

A landscape should nourish the family that lives in it — fresh food, outdoor living, beauty, and a place to gather. We design for how you actually live.

∞

Share the abundance

A well-designed landscape produces more than any family needs. Share the harvest, return surplus to the soil, and let the system sustain itself.

Twelve principles that guide our work

Developed over decades of ecological research and refined through real-world practice. This is the operating system behind every Living Ground landscape.

01

Observe before you act

We spend time on your land before drawing a single line. Sun patterns, wind, water flow, soil biology — the site tells us what it wants to become. Every great design starts with listening.

02

Capture and store energy

Sunlight, rainfall, organic matter — we design systems that catch these resources when they're abundant and bank them for later. Rain gardens, thermal mass, canopy layers that moderate temperature year-round.

03

Every element produces

A tree that only looks good is underperforming. In our designs, every element serves multiple purposes — food, shade, habitat, beauty, mulch. A landscape that works as hard as it looks.

04

Let the system self-correct

Companion planting reduces pests. Ground cover suppresses weeds. Mycorrhizal networks feed the trees. We design intelligence into the landscape so it requires less intervention, not more.

05

Favor what renews

Compost over synthetic fertilizer. Mulch over herbicide. Living ground covers over landscape fabric. We build with biological systems that regenerate themselves season after season.

06

Nothing leaves the system

Pruning becomes mulch. Kitchen scraps become compost. Greywater feeds fruit trees. In a well-designed landscape, every output is an input somewhere else. Waste is a design failure.

07

See the big picture first

Before choosing a single plant, we map the macro patterns — sun arcs, drainage corridors, wind channels, foot traffic. The details fill themselves in once the underlying structure is right.

08

Connect, don't separate

A fruit tree shades a seating area, drops mulch for understory plants, and feeds beneficial insects. We place elements so they support each other — one element, four functions.

09

Start small, build slow

Small systems are easier to maintain and more resilient than large ones. We phase projects so the landscape can establish itself naturally — roots first, then canopy, then abundance.

10

Diversity is resilience

Monocultures fail. Polycultures thrive. We plant dozens of species that fill different ecological niches — so when one struggles, others compensate. The result is a landscape that only gets stronger.

11

Design the edges

The boundary between two systems — sun and shade, wet and dry, garden and wild — is where life concentrates. We design these transitions deliberately, creating the richest zones in your landscape.

12

Design for change

Landscapes evolve. We design for succession — plantings that mature, shift, and improve over decades. Not a static snapshot that degrades, but a living system that compounds in beauty and value.

"The best landscapes don't fight the land — they listen to it."
